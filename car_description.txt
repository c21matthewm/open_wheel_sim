Sim Racing Car Description (IR-18)

The sim racing car should be an IR-18-inspired open-wheel car using real-world spec proportions.

TARGET DIMENSIONS
- Length: 5123 mm
- Width: adjustable by rules envelope, target 1943 mm road-course max
- Height: 1016 mm
- Wheelbase: adjustable 2985-3086 mm, default near 3035 mm for a neutral baseline
- Front wheel size: 15 in diameter x 10 in width
- Rear wheel size: 15 in diameter x 14 in width
- Front tire OD: about 25.5 in
- Rear tire OD: about 27.0 in

VISUAL SHAPE
- Very low nose
- Open wheels clearly separated from body
- Tight, narrow monocoque center section
- Cockpit opening slightly aft of front axle line
- Side profile should be wedge-like and stretched, not chunky
- Rear bodywork compact and tapered
- Use a simple shadow/silhouette first before adding aero detail

PHYSICS TARGET
- Mass target without driver/fuel near 725-740 kg depending on track configuration
- Power band 550-700 hp depending on boost mode
- 6-speed paddle-shift gearbox
- Strong aero dependence above medium speed
- High brake power and short stopping distances
- Quick steering response, fast yaw rotation, but punish over-commitment when aero platform is disturbed
- Road course aero package = higher downforce and drag
- Speedway aero package = lower drag, lower downforce, more pitch/yaw sensitivity in traffic

GAMEPLAY FEEL
- Car should feel nervous when unloaded, planted when speed and aero are present
- Dirty air should reduce front grip semi-noticeably
- Curb strikes should upset platform more than in heavier GT cars
- Throttle application should require precision because power-to-weight is extreme
- Setup changes to wing, ride height, and anti-roll balance should be very noticeableFront wing should look light and efficient, not bulky.
- Keep the assembly low to the ground and visually wide.
- Mainplane should be thin in section.
- Use minimal endplate mass; outer tips should taper and feel blade-like.
- Avoid square boxed ends, thick vertical slabs, or oversized footplates.
- On speedway trim, bias the shape toward a flatter single-plane look.
- On road-course trim, allow more flap depth, but keep the outer ends visually light.Rear wing should be compact, clean, and tensioned.
- Keep the wing visually smaller than a modern F1-style rear wing.
- Supports should be slim and mechanically plausible.
- End treatment should be minimal; do not use oversized slab endplates.
- Speedway trim should move toward a single-plane, no-endplate look.
- Road/street trim can use more flap area, but the whole rear wing should still look light and narrow-edged.
- Rear bodywork and wing should feel integrated, not like a large box sitting on top of the gearbox.
